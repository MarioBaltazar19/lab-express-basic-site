Margaret Hamilton

SAGE Project



From 1961 to 1963, Hamilton worked on the Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE) Project at the MIT Lincoln Lab,

where she was one of the programmers who wrote software for the prototype AN/FSQ-7 computer (the XD-1),

used by the U.S. Air Force to search for possibly unfriendly aircraft. She also wrote software

for a satellite tracking project at the Air Force Cambridge Research Laboratories.


Draper Laboratory



Hamilton was initially hired as a programmer for this process but moved on into system designs. By 1965,

she was in charge of all Command Module software, which was all the software for navigation and lunar landing guidance.

Hamilton's team was responsible for developing in-flight software, which included algorithms designed by

various senior scientists for the Apollo command module, Apollo lunar module, and the subsequent Skylab.


Apollo program



In one of the critical moments of the Apollo 11 mission, the Apollo Guidance Computer, together with the on-board flight software,

averted an abort of the landing on the Moon. Three minutes before the lunar lander reached the Moon's surface,

several computer alarms were triggered. The on-board flight software captured these alarms with the "never supposed to happen displays"

interrupting the astronauts with priority alarm displays.


Businesses



In 1976, Hamilton co-founded with Saydean Zeldin a company called Higher Order Software to further develop ideas about error

prevention and fault tolerance emerging from their experience at MIT working on the Apollo program. They created a product called USE.

IT, based on the HOS methodology they developed at MIT.
